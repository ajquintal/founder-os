# Milpa — Concept Package

**One line.** Milpa moves money from Mexican and Guatemalan immigrants in the US to family back home for a $2.99 (waivable) fee plus a disclosed 1.0–1.5% FX spread — nothing recurring. The product is a transfer; the business is the license stack and BSA/AML program that let the app touch money at all.

**Scope note (read this first).** This is a *strictly transactional* consumer money-services business: no SaaS, no subscription, no MRR, no seats, no software SKU, no value ladder. Revenue = fee + FX spread per transfer. The critical path is compliance and licensure, not software. Several Founder-OS Concept skills assume a digital-product / funnel / warm-outbound motion; where their machinery does not fit, this package adapts the substance and records the mismatch in **Appendix A — Skill-fit friction log**.

## 1. Stress-test verdict

```
BET:        Stand up Milpa as a licensed US MSB (FinCEN + 6-state MTLs, sponsor-bank/FBO, BSA/AML program)
            moving money US→Mexico then US→Guatemala, monetized by a $2.99 fee + 1.0–1.5% FX spread per
            transfer, so that it reaches a ~$1M+ revenue run-rate by end of Year 1 — funded through a
            ~9-month, ~$1–2M pre-revenue licensing/compliance/capital build.

VERDICT:    REFINE

WHY:        The corridor is real, durable, and boring in the best way, and the per-transfer economics
            genuinely close (~53% contribution, LTV/CAC >3x). But as framed this opens a major new
            operating front that does not compound the primary engine (Executive Edge), demands ~$1–2M
            of working capital and ~9 months before a single dollar of *legal* revenue, and — uniquely —
            cannot be de-risked by the usual "sell before you build" test, because transmitting money
            before licensure is a federal crime. Refine the SEQUENCE and the CAPITAL GATE, not the idea:
            prove the two things that can't be assumed (sponsor-bank access and sender demand/CAC) with
            cheap pre-license tests before committing the license-and-float stack.

MAKE-OR-BREAK ASSUMPTIONS:
  1. An MSB-friendly sponsor bank will grant an FBO/pre-funded-float relationship — confidence: L/M —
     cheapest test: secure a signed LOI/term sheet from one (and ideally a redundant second) sponsor
     bank BEFORE any license filing or capital commit. This is existential; MSB "de-risking" is real.
  2. Senders in the 6-state footprint can be acquired at ≤ ~$28 blended CAC and will repeat (~2 transfers/mo)
     — confidence: M — cheapest test: a pre-launch bilingual waitlist + a small geo-targeted ad test
     measuring cost-per-signup (legal pre-license — collecting interest, not transmitting money).
  3. State MTLs + FinCEN registration land inside a ~6–18-month, ~$1–2M envelope — confidence: M —
     cheapest test: regulatory counsel produces a costed, sequenced 6-state licensing + surety-bond +
     net-worth + capital plan before spend is authorized.

FAILURE-MODE FLAGS:  new front (fires hard — a second, unrelated venture concurrent with the primary bet) ·
                     architecture-before-revenue (fires, but is a PARTIAL FALSE POSITIVE: the "architecture"
                     here is legally mandatory licensure, not self-indulgent overbuild — it cannot be skipped
                     or sequenced behind a sale) · founder-dependence (managed — a senior Compliance Officer
                     and Treasury lead are designed in) · future-state bias (managed — scope is deliberately
                     narrow: 2 corridors, 6 states, 1 product).

ECONOMICS:  plausible at the unit level, unproven at the venture level — per-transfer contribution ~$3.93
            (~53%) closes cleanly; venture P&L is EBITDA-negative in Year 1 and reaches break-even only after
            multi-state footprint expansion (Year 2–3). The fixed compliance/licensing/float nut is the story.

GUARDRAILS:  clear as a plan — fully legal (a licensed MSB, not crypto/speculation; the FX spread is a
            disclosed service margin, fully consistent with "no gray areas"). CONTINGENT on: the ~$1–2M
            spend, all banking/license filings, and any fund movement being human-gated with a rollback path;
            all consumer + outbound comms drafts-only; secrets names-only. No medical/legal claims apply.

FOCUS COST:  High and real. Every founder-hour and dollar into Milpa's license stack is off Executive Edge,
            the live engine carrying the $1M-by-EOY-2026 mandate. Milpa does not compound it — it competes
            with it for the scarcest inputs (founder attention + capital).

NEXT:        Run the pre-license evidence bundle (sponsor-bank LOI + waitlist/CAC ad test + counsel's costed
            licensing/capital plan). Kill window: if no sponsor-bank LOI within ~120 days (by ~2026-11-15),
            shelve it — without banking access there is no business, at any capital level.
```

**Why REFINE, not GO or KILL.** The business is sound and legal, so it is not a KILL. But under Tony's own standards — *one primary bet at a time; lean GO only when it compounds an existing engine; economics before build; sell before you build* — a capital-heavy, ~9-month-to-revenue second front that legally *cannot* be sold before it is built does not clear the GO bar as framed. The refinement (gate the capital behind two cheap pre-license proofs, and make it an explicit, resourced second bet) converts it into a clean CONDITIONAL GO at the commit gate (§5).

## 2. Market scan

**Market read.** Cross-border remittance is a large, durable, non-speculative flow. Remittances to low- and middle-income countries reached ~**$685B in 2024** (World Bank, `sourced`). Latin America & the Caribbean received ~**$170B in small-dollar transfers, ~80% of it from the US** (~$136B US→LAC; Inter-American Dialogue, 2025, `sourced`). The **US→Mexico corridor is the single largest bilateral corridor on earth at ~$60–65B/yr** (Banco de México / industry consensus, `benchmarked`), average transfer ~**$350–400** ("most senders remit more than $300," Dialogue; brief models $380, `benchmarked`). **Guatemala** receives ~**$20–21B/yr, ~19–20% of GDP** — among the most remittance-dependent economies on earth — overwhelmingly from the US (World Bank / IMF, `benchmarked`). Channels are now **roughly evenly split digital vs. cash-origin**, with agent-based methods still ~56% (Dialogue, `sourced`). Momentum: high and structural, but the corridor is **brutally price-competed** — the World Bank's US→Mexico data (Aug 2025) shows all-in costs on a $200 send ranging from **−2.44% to 6.90%**, with digital leaders at or below **~1.3%** and some (Xoom, Walmart2World) posting **near-zero or negative** headline cost as an acquisition loss-leader. Regional **weighted-average cost is ~3.0%**, and for large senders **below ~2.4%** (Dialogue, `sourced`). Translation: price transparency and low cost are **table stakes on this exact corridor**, not a differentiator.

**Competitive positioning.**

| Competitor | Price / model (US→MX/LatAm) | What they do | Weakness we exploit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Western Union** | Fee + wide FX spread; all-in often ~4–6%+ on cash | Ubiquitous retail agent + app; "send anywhere" | Junk-fee reputation, opaque rate, dated UX, English-first cash-counter feel |
| **MoneyGram** | Fee + FX spread; retail + app | Retail network (incl. Walmart) + digital | Same opacity/trust deficit; app is a bolt-on, not the product |
| **Remitly** | Fee + FX spread; "Economy" (bank, cheaper/slower) vs "Express" (card, instant, pricier) | Digital-first, **immigrant-first**, strong MX/LatAm incl. cash pickup | Already owns "immigrant-first mobile" at scale + capital — Milpa's positioning overlaps it, not beats it |
| **Xoom (PayPal)** | Fee + FX spread; **near-0% promo on small MX sends** | Digital MX/Guatemala, PayPal balance sheet | Aggressive promo pricing already undercuts an "honest price" wedge; less community/brand intimacy |
| **Wise** | Mid-market rate + transparent flat % (~1.3% observed) | Radical **transparency**, bank-to-bank | Thin on cash pickup + rural LatAm reach; not culturally Latino-native; less fit for underbanked/ITIN senders |
| **Félix Pago** | Fee + FX spread; chat-native | **Bilingual, Latino-first, WhatsApp-native** US→LatAm | Newer/smaller and rails-dependent — but **already occupies Milpa's "bilingual, for-us" wedge** |

**Pricing benchmark.** Observed all-in range on US→Mexico: **~0–1.3% (digital leaders / promos) ↔ ~3% (regional weighted average) ↔ ~4–6%+ (cash-counter incumbents)**. Milpa's modeled **~1.9% all-in take** sits *competitively at the low end* — clearly under cash incumbents and the regional average, but **not below the sharpest digital leaders**, who sometimes price the first small send to zero. Milpa should therefore compete on **all-in transparency + reliability + cultural trust + Guatemala rural payout depth**, not on being the outright cheapest — a race it cannot win against PayPal/Wise balance sheets. Hold the $2.99 fee (waivable) and 1.0–1.5% spread; lead with the guaranteed exact-payout quote, not a "cheapest" claim it can't defend.

**The wedge.** *A Spanish-first, anti-junk-fee remittance built specifically for the Mexican-and-Guatemalan diaspora — pairing an exact, guaranteed-payout quote with deep rural payout reach (SPEI + Banrural) that the transparency-led digital leaders underserve, delivered with bilingual human trust as the product.*

**Riskiest thing the scan could NOT confirm (be honest).** That this wedge is *structurally* defensible. Transparency is already Wise's brand; immigrant-first mobile is already Remitly's; bilingual/Latino-native is already Félix's; and Xoom already prices the on-ramp to ~zero. Milpa's real, defensible moat per the brief is **retention — habit + trust + saved recipients + compliance-grade reliability**, plus **Guatemala/Banrural rural depth** as a genuine niche the digital-only leaders serve thinly. Whether "for-us, exactly-honest, and reliable to the rural last mile" converts and *retains* against incumbents with more capital and near-zero on-ramp pricing is the single unproven assumption — and it is exactly what the pre-license waitlist/CAC test in §1 exists to probe.

## 3. Offer + economics

> **Structural note.** This venture has **one product — a remittance transfer** — so the standard value-ladder artifact (core → order bump → OTO1 → OTO2 → recurring → high-ticket backend) **does not apply**: there is no ascension, no bump, no recurring fee, no SKU. The "offer" is a **product-configuration matrix** (funding × payout × speed) priced by a **fee + FX-spread take rate**. Revenue-per-customer grows only via **frequency and principal**, never via stack/AOV-lift. The economics *gate* (contribution, CAC, LTV, break-even) applies fully and is where the real work is.

**Dream outcome (sender's words, Spanish-first).** *"Mi dinero llega completo y a tiempo — sé exactamente cuánto reciben, y nadie me cobra de más por debajo de la mesa."* ("My money arrives whole and on time — I know exactly how much they get, and no one quietly overcharges me.")

**Offer configuration matrix (the "stack," reframed).**

| Axis | Options | Role | Price/margin logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Funding (pay-in) | ACH bank (default) · debit card (instant, roadmap: RTP) | Cost lever, not a rung | ACH is cheapest COGS (~$0.90); card cost passed through in the customer rate |
| Payout (delivery) | MX: SPEI bank/CLABE · MX cash pickup (Elektra/Bancoppel/OXXO/Telecomm) · GT: Banrural/Banco Industrial bank · GT cash pickup | Reach + cost lever | Bank/SPEI is cheapest; cash pickup carries agent commission — the biggest COGS swing |
| Speed | Minutes (default promise) · Economy (lower spread, slower) | Value/price tier | Economy trades a thinner spread for slower settlement |
| Pricing | $2.99 fixed fee (waivable; first transfer free) + 1.0–1.5% FX spread (model 1.3%), fully disclosed | The entire monetization | Blended realized fee ~$2.50 after promos; all-in take ~1.9% |
| Acquisition offer | First transfer free + boosted rate; give-$10 / get-$10 referral credits | Deliberate loss-leader | The first transfer is contribution-*negative* by design (see self-liquidation, below) |

**Economics gate (per average $380 transfer).**

| Line | Per txn | Tag |
|---|---:|---|
| Revenue — fixed fee (blended) | $2.50 | `benchmarked` |
| Revenue — FX spread @ 1.3% | $4.94 | `benchmarked` |
| **Gross revenue** | **$7.44** (~1.9% take) | `benchmarked` |
| Variable COGS (pay-in + payout + FX slippage + screening + fraud reserve + network) | ($3.51) | `benchmarked` |
| **Contribution** | **$3.93 (~53%)** | `benchmarked` |

- **Affordable CPA.** First-year contribution/active sender ≈ 24 transfers × $3.93 ≈ **$94** (`benchmarked`; 2 transfers/mo). To hold **first-year LTV/CAC ≥ 3×**, CPA ceiling ≈ **~$31**; on an 18-month contribution (~$140) the ceiling rises to **~$47**. Brief's **~$28** blended CAC target sits under both — headroom exists. One-time KYC/IDV adds **$1.50–$2.50** per new customer (`benchmarked`), effectively raising true acquisition cost to ~$30–48.
- **LTV.** ~**$94/yr contribution**; remittance is a multi-year, habitual behavior, so 3-year LTV ≈ **~$230–280** net of churn (`assumed`). **LTV/CAC ≈ 3.4× first-year, >5× lifetime** (`benchmarked/assumed`). Retention (habit + trust + saved recipients) is the moat, not switching cost.
- **Payback.** CAC (~$28) + first-transfer subsidy (waived fee + boosted rate + referral credits, ~$15–20) ≈ **$45–48 acquisition cost**, recovered at $3.93/transfer in **~12 transfers ≈ 5–6 months** at 2/mo (`assumed`). Consistent with "a few months."
- **Break-even (venture level).** At ~$3.93 contribution, covering a **~$150k/mo fixed nut** (Compliance Officer + Treasury + small product/eng + counsel + AML audit + bond premiums + hosting) needs **~38,000 transfers/mo ≈ ~16,000 active senders**; a **~$250k/mo** nut needs **~64,000 transfers/mo ≈ ~27,000 active** (`assumed`, order-of-magnitude — fixed cost not fully specified in brief). Exit-Year-1 is ~8,000 active → **structurally pre-break-even in Year 1**; break-even is a **footprint-expansion (Year 2–3) milestone**, not a Year-1 event. This matches the brief's EBITDA-negative Year-1 call.

**Self-liquidation read (inverted).** There is **no self-liquidating front-end offer** because there is no front-end *sale* — the first transfer is a deliberate **loss-leader** (fee waived, rate boosted). "Self-liquidation" reframes as **acquisition payback in transfers**: the on-ramp runs contribution-negative on transfer #1 and pays back over transfers #2–#12. Scaling is "free" only once blended CAC + subsidy is comfortably below cumulative per-sender contribution — i.e., once retention is proven.

**The one number the whole model is most sensitive to.** **Funding + payout mix (COGS), not conversion or retention.** The brief is explicit: a debit-heavy funding mix or a cash-heavy payout mix "can halve contribution." Steering senders toward **ACH funding + SPEI/bank payout** (vs. debit + cash pickup) is the single biggest operational lever on unit economics. Protect the mix.

**Implementation priority (inverted from the usual "sell the top rung first").** You legally cannot sell before licensure, so the sequence is: **(1)** sponsor-bank + FBO and one Mexico payout rail (SPEI aggregator) secured; **(2)** first-state MTL + FinCEN registration live; **(3)** a tightly-scoped **Mexico-only, one-state, ACH-funded / bank-payout pilot** (the margin-protective configuration) to prove reliability + repeat rate; **(4)** widen states and add cash pickup + Guatemala/Banrural only after unit economics and reliability hold. Do **not** build the 6-state, dual-corridor, all-payout cathedral before the first pilot transfers clear.

But demand is unproven, the wedge is contested by better-capitalized incumbents, and — decisively — the venture cannot be tested by a manual sale (pre-license transmission is illegal). The disciplined move is to **fund the two cheap pre-license proofs (sponsor-bank access + demand/CAC) and the costed capital plan**, and to hold the ~$1–2M license-and-float commit behind them. The ~2026-10-15 / ~2026-11-15 gates convert this into a clean capital commit or a clean reallocation.

## Appendix A — Skill-fit friction log (Concept-stage hardening)

Where the Founder-OS Concept skills assumed software/SaaS/subscription/funnel/warm-outbound patterns that did not fit a strictly-transactional, licensed-MSB consumer business. Skill · what broke.

1. **stress-test — "cheapest test = a conversation, a landing page, a manual sale; never build it and see" / "sell before you build."** Structurally impossible for an MSB: transmitting money before FinCEN registration + state MTLs is a federal crime (unlicensed money transmission, 18 USC 1960). The skill's entire cheapest-test / sell-before-build spine assumes you can transact pre-build. The real pre-license "tests" are a sponsor-bank LOI, a payout-aggregator term sheet, and a waitlist/CAC ad test — none of which are sales. Had to substitute.
2. **stress-test — failure-mode "architecture before revenue" cannot distinguish legally-mandated licensure from Tony's overbuild pattern.** The flag fires, but here the "architecture" is unavoidable regulatory licensure, not a self-indulgent cathedral. The skill offers no way to mark a fired trap as an externally-imposed false positive; it needs operator judgment the rubric doesn't scaffold.
3. **stress-test / decision-standards — "kill window default 30 days for a revenue play" / "not tracking to revenue within its window."** A licensed MSB has a ~6–18-month licensing critical path before *any* legal revenue. A 30-day revenue kill window is meaningless; kill gates must be milestone-based (sponsor-bank LOI, license granted), not revenue-by-day-30. The default cadence assumes fast-to-revenue digital plays.
4. **market-scan — "pull real prices from pricing pages / plans; capture the model (one-time / subscription / usage)."** Remittance pricing is a per-transaction **fee + FX spread** that varies by corridor, amount, funding method, and speed, and the FX-spread markup is typically **not published** — you must reverse-engineer effective take rate from a live send quote. The "pricing page / plan / subscription / usage" taxonomy has no clean slot for a variable take-rate; the competitor table's "price/model" column had to be reframed.
5. **market-scan (and opportunity-size) — "TAM = full category revenue."** In remittances the cited number is **GMV/flow** ($80B corridor), while category *revenue* is the ~1.9–3.7% fee+spread pool (~$1.5–3B). The skill doesn't distinguish flow from revenue; naive use overstates TAM ~20–50×. Had to state both explicitly.
6. **market-scan — "the wedge = the one thing none of them ship that this venture can own."** This lens presumes a *shippable product/feature* differentiator. Milpa's actual moat is **retention (habit/trust/reliability) + compliance-grade dependability + Guatemala rural payout depth** — execution and trust, not a feature no one ships. Worse, the obvious feature-wedges (transparency, immigrant-first, bilingual) are already occupied by Wise/Remitly/Félix. The skill over-promises structural defensibility for a business whose moat isn't a feature.
7. **offer-architect — the entire value-ladder artifact (core → order bump → OTO1 → OTO2 → recurring → high-ticket backend) does not apply.** One product (a transfer), no ascension, no bumps, explicitly no recurring fee, no SKU/tier. The skill has archetypes for services, product/commerce, and SaaS/subscription — but **no transactional / marketplace-take-rate archetype**. Its closest ("recurring") is actively wrong here (there is no recurring fee). Had to replace the ladder with a product-configuration matrix.
8. **offer-architect — "AOV target and how the stack lifts it."** There is no stack to lift AOV; revenue-per-customer grows only via **transfer frequency and principal size**, which is a repeat-purchase dynamic, not an AOV-lift mechanism. The "~10× AOV" heuristic is correctly hedged as funnel-only, but the underlying AOV-lift concept has no analog and had to be dropped.
9. **offer-architect — "self-liquidating front-end" inverts.** The first transfer is deliberately **contribution-negative** (fee waived + boosted rate + referral credits as an acquisition subsidy). There is no front-end offer that pays for acquisition; payback happens over transfers #2–N. "Self-liquidation" had to be reframed as "acquisition payback in transfers."
10. **offer-architect — "Implementation priority: sell the highest rung manually before building the funnel."** No rungs exist, and manual selling is illegal pre-license. Priority had to invert to: secure banking + one payout rail + first license, then run a margin-protective pilot. (The economics-gate half of the skill — contribution/CAC/LTV/break-even — fit *well*; the offer-structure half did not.)
11. **offer-architect — "the one number the model is most sensitive to."** The skill's worked examples answer with ascension/retention metrics (install→retainer conversion; net revenue retention). Here the most-sensitive number is **COGS mix** (ACH-vs-debit funding, bank-vs-cash payout) — a cost-side lever the sensitivity framing doesn't anticipate.
12. **opportunity-size — "reachable buyers come from real reach — owned audience, warm list, outbound capacity — not 1% of the market."** Milpa is a **cold-start consumer app** with no owned audience, no warm list, and no outbound motion; reachable buyers = f(ad spend, blended CAC, referral virality). The skill is built for founder-led warm/outbound motions (its worked example: "30–50 warm companies from LinkedIn") and offers no scaffold for paid-acquisition consumer sizing.
13. **opportunity-size — "billing frequency: one-time, or recurring × months" and "price/ACV."** Remittance is neither one-time nor subscription-recurring; it's **variable high-frequency repeat purchase** (1–4×/month, habitual) on a **variable principal** with a per-transaction take (no ACV). A single "billing frequency" multiplier and an "ACV" field don't capture a repeat-purchase frequency distribution; the formula had to be adapted.
14. **opportunity-size — "SAM = TAM × servable geo × ICP × channel," treated as soft %s.** For an MSB the servable geo is a **hard legal gate** — you may only serve states where the MTL is granted — not a soft market-fit percentage. The state-by-state license constraint governs SOM more than competition or reach do, which the multiplier model underweights.
15. **go-no-go — "fund the cheapest evidence step — a manual sale, not a build" / "a build-first milestone with no demand test is a NO-GO tell."** The first evidence step cannot be a sale (pre-license illegality), and the mandatory licensing build is not a self-indulgent architecture play. Applied literally, the gate would wrongly flag legally-required licensure as the architecture-before-revenue anti-pattern. Had to distinguish "mandatory regulated build" from "overbuild."
16. **go-no-go / decision-standards — the "building-ahead-of-demand" exception doesn't cover capital-intensive regulated builds.** Its four conditions ("it's the AI's/operator's time not the founder's"; "demo-scoped / feature-flagged so live flows are untouched") are nonsensical for a ~$1–2M license + float + surety-bond commitment, which is neither the AI's time nor feature-flaggable. The exception framework assumes software builds.
17. **go-no-go — composition tension.** The method says "an unresolved REFINE must be resolved before this gate," yet the REFINE here is inherently about capital-gating / new-front — exactly what the gate adjudicates via CONDITIONAL GO ("fund the test, not the build"). Slightly circular; the skill gives no guidance for gating a venture whose refinement is "prove the un-cheaply-testable." Resolved by treating the CONDITIONAL-GO conditions as the refinement.
18. **Cross-cutting (all five skills) — "load the active ventures/<slug>/venture-context.md."** Milpa's context lives as a venture *brief* (00-venture-idea.md), not a `ventures/<slug>/venture-context.md` produced by the /new-venture intake. Minor, but every skill's load-first step assumes the intake artifact already exists at a path that isn't populated in this eval.

*What fit well (so the log is calibrated, not just complaints):* the value-equation numerator (dream outcome), the contribution/CAC/LTV/payback economics gate, the booked-vs-collected cash discipline, the tops-down/bottoms-up reconciliation logic, the guardrail check (legality, drafts-only, secrets, human-gated irreversibles), and the stress-test → go-no-go composition arc all applied cleanly. The friction is concentrated in the **offer-structure (value-ladder/AOV), the sell-before-build/cheapest-test spine, and the warm-reach sizing basis** — i.e., wherever a skill hard-codes a SaaS/funnel/founder-led-outbound assumption.
