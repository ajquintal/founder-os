# /stress-test — Full method & rubric

The working detail behind the SKILL.md summary. One level deep; does not fan out further.

## Step 1 — Restate the bet (one sentence)

Force the idea into: **"[action] so that [claimed outcome], within [timeframe]."** If it can't be stated that cleanly, the idea is too fuzzy to evaluate — sharpen it first. Strip adjectives; name the actual move and the actual result.

## Step 2 — Governing filter

Ask: **does this compound wealth, or does it feel productive while diluting focus?** Hold the answer lightly here; steps 3–7 test it. But note the first instinct — it's usually right.

## Step 3 — Make-or-break assumptions (≤3)

List the smallest set of things that must ALL be true for the bet to work. For each:
- **Confidence:** H (evidence exists) / M (plausible, untested) / L (hope).
- **Cheapest test:** the fastest, cheapest way to find out — a conversation, a landing page, a manual sale, a spreadsheet. Never "build it and see."

If the #1 assumption is Low confidence and the cheapest test is expensive, that alone can force a REFINE (shrink the bet to make the test cheap) or KILL.

## Step 4 — Red-team (argue for KILL)

Take the strongest skeptic's seat and attack:
- **Demand:** who actually pays, and is there evidence, or is it assumed?
- **Economics:** does the math close (step 6)?
- **Focus:** what does this steal attention from — especially the current primary bet?
- **Timing:** why now? What breaks if it waits a quarter?
- **Competition/moat:** what's the wedge? If none, why does this win?
- **Reversibility:** if it fails, what's the cost to unwind?

## Step 5 — Failure-mode filter (Tony-specific; auto-flag)

Flag every trap that fires — these are the documented patterns:

| Trap | The tell |
|---|---|
| **New front** | It adds a concurrent business/offer/audience while another is unfinished. |
| **Architecture before revenue** | It's building/infrastructure justified before a paying customer exists. |
| **Standards-as-bottleneck** | It can't ship until it meets a bar higher than the market requires. |
| **Future-state bias** | It designs the end-state before the core is proven. |
| **Founder-dependence** | It structurally needs Tony in the loop forever. |

A fired trap is not an automatic KILL — but it must be named, and the verdict must explain how the bet avoids or accepts it.

## Step 6 — Economics gate

At minimum, sanity-check: affordable CPA, target AOV, break-even, first-year LTV (see `operating-playbooks.md`). Three outcomes:
- **plausible** — the math closes on conservative assumptions.
- **unproven** — the math *could* close but rests on an untested number → that number becomes a make-or-break assumption.
- **doesn't close** — the math fails even generously → KILL or REFINE the model.

## Step 7 — Guardrail check (can override everything)

Check against `guardrails.md`. Any of these makes the verdict **KILL or escalate**, regardless of upside:
- LYV firewall breach (sourcing from co-owned relationships/data).
- Risk to the job, other ventures, or reputation.
- Legal/compliance exposure; medical or legal claims without review.
- Would require sending/publishing without Tony's approval.

## Step 8 — Verdict thresholds

- **GO** — compounds an existing engine or has evidenced demand; economics plausible; no fired trap left unmanaged; guardrails clear. Give the single next action + a kill window.
- **REFINE** — real value but flawed as framed: it opens a front, the economics are unproven, or it's overbuilt. Return the specific narrowing/sequencing that would turn it into a GO.
- **KILL** — a guardrail conflict, economics that don't close, or it's mostly architecture-before-revenue / a distraction from the primary bet. Name the fatal reason in one line.

Always state the **focus cost** — what a yes displaces — even on a GO.
